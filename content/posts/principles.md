Excellent programmers are excellent problem solvers. Thus, helping students become problem solvers is one of my top priorities. Getting comfortable reasoning abstractly and learning how to manage the complexity inherit in writing large programs are two of the greatest challenges new programmers face. It is my view that anyone is capable of solving difficult problems provided they develop habits. The specific behaviors needed to excel in a given field will vary. Though, in general, efficient practice over time leads to mastery.

## Make learning enjoyable

People do things that are enjoyable, for better or for worse. I believe making learning and problem solving enjoyable is absolutely vital. Unfortunately, many students are averse to practicing solving problems. It is useful to consider that the same students are able to spend enormous amounts of time and energy doing other activities such as playing video games or chatting on social media. Such activities are easier for them to do because the act of doing the activities is rewarding and enjoyable. If students make an effort to find ways to develop positive emotions with their studies than they will naturally spend more learning.

## Grow tiny habits into big ones

A tiny habit is a term coined by behavior change researcher, BJ Fogg. It refers to a scaled down version of a goal or aspiration. Tiny habits are specific, easy to do and lead to big changes. As he puts it, "tiny habits are the compound interest of behavior change".

I help my students develop tiny habits related to the aspiration of being a problem solver and learner. This approach has helped many of my students go from not practicing coding at all to practicing everyday. The skill of creating effective tiny habits is perhaps the most valuable skill I teach my students.

For more information on this topic please see Tiny Habits: The Small Changes that Change by BJ Fogg.

## Review

I review old concepts with students whenever it becomes clear to me that there are gaps in their understanding. For students who are interested, I can teach them how
develop an effective review system of their own using spaced repetition software.

## Be flexible

I recognize that people have unique learning styles and try to understand how each of my students learns best. I make an effort to explain the same idea from multiple angles. I believe that viewing the same idea from multiple perspectives is an effective method for deeply understanding something. Also, by teaching in this way I encourage the student to approach their learning in the same fashion.

## Work on Interesting Material

People learn best when they study things that they feel are important. Based on this, I have endeavored to find teaching materials that are interesting and lots of fun. I am in great debt to the creators of How to Design Programs as they have created a curriculum that is both comprehensive and fun. Still, even within this curriculum a students interests may vary. I try to be sensitive to this fact and avoid "you have to"s at all costs. If a student seems bored with a particular problem, I am happy to either move on or come up with a related but more interesting problem. On the other hand, if a student seems really interested in a particular problem we can spend more time on it. Learning is very personal and students will learn best when the material they are working from is of interest to them.

An advantage to using BSL and Pyret is that both languages are accompanied by online, freely available, textbooks. The projects in these books mostly involve creating programs with a graphical user interface. These programs include things like games and desktop applications. These types of programs are much more fun for students to create than the typical command line batch program that are so commonly taught to beginner programmers.
